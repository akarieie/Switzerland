The Matterhorn

Switzerland's most famous peak, rising, 478 meters above sea level

The Matterhorn is arguably the most recognizable mountain in the world, renowned for its almost perfectly symmetrical, pyramidal shape. Towering at 4,478 meters (14,692 ft), it stands as an indelible emblem of the Swiss Alps, straddling the border between Switzerland and Italy. The legendary, car-free village of Zermatt, nestled in the Matter Valley, serves as the exclusive Swiss base for all Matterhorn adventures.
The Matterhorn was the last major peak of the Alps to be conquered, holding out until July 14, 1865. On that day, a team led by the British explorer Edward Whymper successfully completed the first ascent via the Hörnli ridge. However, triumph quickly turned to tragedy when four of the seven climbers fell to their deaths on the descent after a rope snapped. This disaster gained worldwide attention, instantly transforming Zermatt from a remote farming community into a global center for tourism and alpine history. The story is preserved today in the Matterhorn Museum – Zermatlantis.
The Matterhorn region offers unparalleled natural beauty and accessible alpine adventure. Its unique silhouette makes it the most photographed mountain globally, with famous views often including its reflection in the Riffelsee Lake.

Visitors can access spectacular viewpoints without climbing:
  • Gornergrat Bahn: A scenic cogwheel railway leading to the Gornergrat ridge (3,089m) for panoramic views of the Matterhorn and dozens of other surrounding 4,000-meter peaks.
  • Matterhorn Glacier Paradise: Europe's highest 3S cableway takes guests up to 3,883m for close-up glacier views and year-round snow activities.

The preserved charm of the quiet, car-free Zermatt village enhances the experience, offering a relaxing and historically rich base for exploring the monumental surroundings.

Key Facts

  • Location: Zermatt, Canton of Valais
  • Elevation: 4,478 m (14,692 ft)
  • Best Viewpoint: Gornergrat
